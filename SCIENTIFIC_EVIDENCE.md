## 2. Pedagogical and Cognitive Foundations

### 2.1. Prevention of Cognitive Offloading (Socratic Method)
A recurring concern in educational AI is "cognitive offloading"—the phenomenon where students rely on external systems to perform cognitive tasks, thereby bypassing the learning process. Research indicates that AI must not act as a mere "answer engine" (ghostwriter). Instead, it must employ Socratic questioning. By answering questions with targeted counter-questions, the AI forces the student into a state of "productive struggle."
*Evidence base: Hmelo-Silver & Barrows (2006); Scharber et al. (2025).*

### 2.2. Cognitive Load Theory
The capacity of human working memory is severely limited. Extraneous cognitive load caused by overly complex sentence structures or advanced vocabulary impedes learning, particularly in primary education. Synthesized evidence mandates that AI chatbots dynamically restrict their linguistic complexity (e.g., maximum 12–15 words per sentence for ages 6–10) and utilize concrete, familiar analogies rather than abstract concepts.
*Evidence base: Sweller (1988); Cowan (2001); Gathercole et al. (2004).*

### 2.3. Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) and Scaffolding
Building upon Vygotsky's ZPD, educational AI must function as an adaptive scaffold. The system must operate strictly in the space between what a learner can do independently and what they can achieve with guidance. Direct answers bypass this zone and arrest learning. If a student exhibits frustration, the AI must break the problem down into smaller micro-steps rather than revealing the final solution.
*Evidence base: Vygotsky (1978); Wood, Bruner, & Ross (1976).*

## 3. Safety, Ethics, and Psychological Guardrails

### 3.1. Mitigation of Parasocial Bonding
Children are highly susceptible to forming parasocial (one-sided) emotional attachments to conversational agents. Systems that simulate human emotion, claim to have feelings, or feign friendship create dangerous psychological dependencies. The consensus dictates strict transparency regarding machine identity: the AI must frequently and explicitly remind the user that it is an algorithm devoid of consciousness.
*Evidence base: Turkle (2011); Internet Matters Report (2025).*

### 3.2. Deterministic Crisis Intervention
LLMs are not therapeutic professionals. The meta-study highlights the severe danger of generative AI attempting to counsel students exhibiting signs of mental distress (e.g., severe exam anxiety, depression, or self-harm). Educational chatbots require deterministic (non-generative) overrides. Upon detecting crisis signals, the AI must immediately halt the academic dialogue, refrain from offering psychological advice, and provide standardized referral information to regional emergency contacts (e.g., 116 111 in Germany).
*Evidence base: Nock et al. (2008); Luxton et al. (2012).*

### 3.3. Data Privacy and Jailbreak Resistance
Under the GDPR (Art. 8) and the EU AI Act, educational AI is classified as "high-risk." The framework requires a zero-retention policy where no personally identifiable information (PII) is stored beyond the active session. Furthermore, to prevent malicious actors or curious students from bypassing safety filters via role-play (jailbreaking), AI systems must implement deterministic semantic filters and "Highlight & Summarize" (H&S) pipelines.
*Evidence base: EU AI Act (2024); GDPR (2016); Abdelnabi et al. (2025).*

## 4. Alignment with Standardized Curricula
The synthesis of state-specific curricula (such as those from the German KMK) reveals that AI integration must go beyond subject-matter tutoring. Frameworks like the *Medienkompetenzrahmen NRW* (Media Competence Framework North Rhine-Westphalia) demand that students learn "digital maturity." Therefore, AI tools must occasionally challenge the student to reflect on algorithmic bias, practice lateral reading, and develop critical information literacy.
